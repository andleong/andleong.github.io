Compoundi helps you easily calculate your potential future wealth using the power of compound interest!

This tool is perfect for finding out the future value of any of your assets, including your property, term deposits, bonds and shares...really anything that changes in value over time!

    All you need is:
  • The value of your asset today
  • The number of periods you will hold the asset for (in days, months, or years)
  • Your expected yearly return on the asset (note: you can freely estimate this if it is unknown!)

Here are some powerful ways to use Compoundi

You currently own a house worth $500,000 and are curious as to how much it could be worth in 20 years. Your research tells you that real estate in your area averages a 5% increase in value year on year.

To calculate, we would enter "500000" into Amount, "20" into Time, and "5" into Interest.

That should give us $1,326,648,85!

However, your friend who works in real estate tells you that the assumption that your property value will grow by 5% per year is slightly off and should be 4.9%, but assures that it doesn't make much of a difference. Out of curiosity you decide to enter "4.9" into Interest instead of "5"...

...giving us a value of $1,301,606.68. A difference of $25,042.17!

This goes to show just how sensitive your future value is to your estimates of yearly return - especially when you are looking far into the future.

This example also shows just how important it is to do your own research and come up with an educated estimate of your asset's growth rate. We also recommend that you test out a range of estimates for Amount, Time, and Interest to give you a possible range that your asset value could fall under. Even the best of us aren't fortune tellers!

Knowing the power of compound interest, you decided to deposit $1000 into a savings account with your bank exactly 2 years ago and have not touched it since. You are curious about how much your savings have grown on an interest rate of 1% p.a.

We enter "1000" into Amount, "2" into Time, and "1" into Interest.

The calculator tells us that we now have $1020.10 in savings. An increase of $20.10 from not doing much is pretty good!

However, does this mean that we can buy $20.10 worth of more things with our 2 years of savings? Not exactly. We have to consider the effects of inflation which reduces the value of the spending power of each unit of currency over time! This is unfortunately a natural byproduct of growth in all economies and affects all of us. You may be aware of this force when you notice the price of everything has increased since you were young.

Doing a quick search of your country's inflation rate from your central bank's website, you can see that it was on average 1.5% p.a. for the 2 years that your money was deposited.

Remember the $1020.10 we calculated earlier? That is known as the nominal value of your money because it does not consider inflation. The quoted interest rate by your bank of 1% p.a. is similarly called the nominal interest rate. Now we have to adjust our calculation to take into account inflation.

Inflation has a negative effect on the growth of our money because it is a concept that describes the shrinking of the value of our money. Recall that we found an inflation rate of 1.5% p.a. for the past 2 years, meaning no matter what we do with our money, it will be shrinking in real value by 1.5% p.a.. This time we enter We enter "1000" into Amount, "2" into Time, and "-0.5" into Interest because our money earns interest of 1% p.a. but also shrinks in spending power by 1.5% p.a.

This gives us the current real value of $990.03!

So although our bank statement would say that we grew our money by $20.10, in actuality if we were to spend that money today, we would notice that we couldn't buy almost $10 as much as we could 2 years ago. Although it may sound like a terrible idea to put money into a bank account that pays a rate lower than inflation, imagine if you decided to keep your money somewhere safe at home which earns you no interest!

Our example here hopefully has made you aware that your money will always have the negative force of inflation working against you no matter what you do, assuming that your economy is growing. It is incredibly important, then, that you keep your money earning more money for you. Be even more aware that this does not necessarily mean to find investments that protentially provide an incredible return far beyond the inflation rate. A higher rate of return almost certainly is to make up for the investment's higher risk of not giving what you are expecting. It then comes down to what you personally are willing to lose in order to earn more.
